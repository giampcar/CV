Introduction

Definition

Good distribution practices (GDP) describes what a wholesale distributor must meet to ensure that quality and integrity of medicines are maintained throughout the supply chain.
Compliance with GDP ensures that:
  • Medicines in the supply chain are authorised in accordance with European Union (EU) legislation.
  • Medicines are stored in the right conditions at all times, including during transportation.
  • Contamination by or of other products is avoided.
  • An adequate turnover of stored medicines takes place.
  • The right products reach the right addressee within a satisfactory time period.
  • The distributor should put a tracing system in place to enable finding faulty products and an effective recall procedure.
GDP also applies to the sourcing, storage and transportation of Medicinal Products, active Pharmaceutical Excipients, APIs, Blood Products, and Veterinary Medicinal Products. A wholesale distributor can be: a Pharmaceutical / Biopharmaceutical Company, a Pharmaceutical Wholesaler, a Supply Chain Company, a Subcontracted Logistics Company or a Freight/Parcel Transport Company.

Regulatory References

  • Guidelines on Good Distribution Practice of Medicinal Products for Human Use (2013/C343/01)

Background, and bilateral agreements between Switzerland and the EU

The European Commission's Directorate General for Health and Consumer Policy published the final version of the 'Guideline on Good Distribution Practices of Medicinal Products for Human Use' on 05 November 2013. The Federal Department of Home Affairs has adapted the Guidelines on Good Distribution Practice of Medicinal Products according to EU law. This move increases quality requirements for the distribution of medicinal products and strengthens patient protection.
